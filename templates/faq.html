Frequently Asked Questions

Find answers to common questions about studying in the UK and Ireland

Welcome to our FAQ page—your go-to resource for clear, reliable answers about studying in the United Kingdom (UK) and the Republic of Ireland. Here, we address the most common questions international students have about studying in the UK and Ireland.

Whether you're exploring academic options, understanding cost differences, or navigating your study plans with confidence, this section is designed to provide straightforward guidance and help you make informed decisions every step of the way.

There are three main university intakes in the UK. The biggest one is the Main (Autumn/Fall) Intake, with classes beginning in September or October. The general deadline for applying for undergraduate programmes through UCAS is 15 January, while competitive courses—such as Medicine, Dentistry, Veterinary Science, and all Oxford or Cambridge programmes—require applications by 15 October of the previous year. Postgraduate applications are submitted directly to universities and typically stay open from October to June, though competitive programmes may close earlier.

The second option is the January/February (winter) intake, which is smaller and mostly offers taught postgraduate courses. Applications usually open around August and close between October and November.

The third option, the Spring/Summer Intake (April or May), is the least common. Only a small number of universities offer programmes during this period, mainly at the postgraduate level, and application windows usually run from late autumn to early spring.

In Ireland, universities generally offer two main intakes—autumn and spring. The Autumn (September) Intake is the largest and the standard entry point for both undergraduate and postgraduate programmes. The deadline for applying through the CAO is usually around 1 February, although international students often apply directly to the university and may have later deadlines extending into the summer.

For postgraduate September entry, most applications open in October or November of the previous year, with typical deadlines falling between April and May. It's important for you to apply early because competitive courses fill quickly.

The spring (January) intake is much more limited and is mainly available for selected postgraduate programmes. These applications are usually rolling, with deadlines landing between September and November of the previous year.

For both the UK and Ireland, you need to also remember to factor in the time needed after receiving an offer—this includes accepting the offer, paying any required fees, and completing the student visa process, which typically takes 3 to 6 months.

The best time to start preparing your application is about 12–18 months before your course begins. Starting early really helps—especially if you're aiming for competitive programmes or scholarships and may need plenty of time for visa processing. In the UK, undergraduate applications are submitted through UCAS, which opens in September each year. Ideally, you should begin researching universities and preparing for English-language tests around a year in advance. Most courses have a major deadline in late January, but top universities—and all medicine or dentistry programmes—require applications by mid-October.

For Ireland, applications go through the Central Applications Office (CAO). The system opens in early November, with a normal deadline in early February. Even though that gives some time, applying early is still the smarter choice because admissions operate on a rolling basis, and offers usually come out between May and August. Across both systems, early applications make it easier to meet academic and language requirements, find scholarships and housing, and prepare for your visa process. Once you accept an offer, plan to begin your visa application right away—usually 2–3 months before classes start, depending on processing times.

Choosing the right course and university as an international student can feel overwhelming, but it becomes much easier when you start with a clear, honest look at yourself—your academic background, career ambitions, learning style, budget, visa plans, and language readiness. Once you understand what you need, you can compare your options more confidently.

Undergraduate programmes are usually broader and more structured, often with opportunities like placement years. Postgraduate courses, on the other hand, are typically more specialised, fast-paced, and may include research components. Looking closely at module lists, assessment styles, and any special requirements—such as portfolios or prerequisite subjects—will help you work out whether a course truly suits your strengths and long-term goals.

When choosing a university, try to look beyond overall rankings. Pay attention to the strengths of individual departments, accreditation, graduate success rates, and the style of teaching—whether it's at a Russell Group university, a post-92 institution, or an Irish university or institute. Consider practical factors too, such as the cost of living, location, student support services, and the general campus environment. These day-to-day elements can shape your experience just as much as the academics.

By creating a shortlist, comparing options using consistent criteria, and taking advantage of online or in-person events, you'll be able to choose the course and university that best fit your goals, preferences, and future plans.

To study in the UK or Ireland, you usually need to prepare a set of documents that prove your academic background, identity, English ability, and financial readiness. The exact requirements depend on whether you're applying for an undergraduate or postgraduate course.

For undergraduate programmes, universities typically ask for your official high-school transcripts, predicted grades if you haven't finished school yet, and a school-leaving qualification such as A-Levels, the IB, or the equivalent from your home country. Some universities may also request standardised test scores, depending on the programme.

For postgraduate courses, you'll normally need your bachelor's degree certificate, full university transcripts, and sometimes a document explaining your grading system. Certain courses—especially research-focused ones—may require extra materials such as a research proposal or an academic writing sample. Many postgraduate programmes also ask for a CV.

Across both levels, you'll usually need to provide a valid passport and proof of English language proficiency (like IELTS, TOEFL, or PTE), unless you previously studied in English, as well as supporting documents such as a personal statement or statement of purpose. Academic or professional references are also a common requirement, and creative programmes often ask for a portfolio.

Once you receive an offer, you'll likely need to submit financial and visa-related documents—such as proof of funds, sponsorship letters, medical or health-insurance documents for Ireland, and TB test results if required for UK visas. In some cases, universities may ask for additional items like certified translations, name-change documents, or passport-style photos.

If you're applying to a university in the UK or the Republic of Ireland as an international student, you'll usually need to show proof of your English ability. You can do this by taking a recognised English test, unless you qualify for an exemption.

You'll typically need to take a test if English isn't your first language, if you haven't studied recently in an English-speaking country, if your previous education wasn't fully taught and assessed in English, or if the programme you're applying to requires a higher level of language proficiency.

Common exemptions where you don't need to take an English test include being a citizen of a country that the UK Home Office classifies as majority English-speaking, completing a full degree or several years of secondary school in English in an approved English-speaking country, or holding certain qualifications—such as IB English.

If you do need to sit a test, universities generally accept several options: IELTS Academic, TOEFL iBT, Cambridge English exams (C1 Advanced or C2 Proficiency), PTE Academic, the Duolingo English Test (which is especially common in Ireland), and Trinity ISE for UK visa purposes.

Yes, you can apply for undergraduate or postgraduate programmes in the UK or Ireland even if you haven't received your final academic results yet. Universities do this all the time — they look at things like predicted grades, provisional transcripts, or a letter confirming when you're expected to finish your current studies. If they like your application, they'll give you a conditional offer, which later becomes unconditional once you submit your official final results and meet the academic requirements. The best approach is to apply early with the strongest documents you have available, send your final results as soon as they're issued, and double-check deadlines for academic documents and English-language proof. If there's any chance your final results might be delayed, it's important to contact the admissions team as early as possible to let them know.

The first and most important step in getting your qualifications recognised is to check the entry requirements listed on each university's official course page. Most universities already have internal equivalency charts for common international qualifications, so you may find that your credentials are already understood and accepted without extra steps.

After confirming what the university expects, you'll need to gather your key academic documents — typically your Final Award Certificate or Diploma, your official transcripts, and certified translations if they aren't already in English. These documents are uploaded directly through the application platform you're using, whether that's UCAS or the university's own admissions portal.

Once submitted, the Admissions Office will assess your qualifications against local academic standards. They often rely on national guidance for equivalencies, but if your qualification is less common or needs formal verification, they may ask for an additional document: a Statement of Comparability from UK ENIC (formerly UK NARIC) for UK universities, or they may refer you to the NARIC Ireland (QQI) Foreign Qualifications Database for Irish applications.

To apply for undergraduate programmes, you need to have completed secondary education with qualifications equivalent to UK A-levels or the Irish Leaving Certificate, and if you don't meet the requirement, you may choose a foundation or pathway programme to prepare for entry. Applications for undergraduate study are submitted through UCAS in the UK—where most courses share a mid-January deadline and highly competitive fields such as medicine, dentistry, and veterinary science close even earlier—or through the CAO system in Ireland. Postgraduate applications, on the other hand, are submitted directly to each university's online portal, with deadlines varying widely and many programmes accepting applicants on a rolling basis. While undergraduate submissions usually include school transcripts, a single academically focused personal statement, one reference, and proof of English proficiency, postgraduate applications include degree transcripts, a more detailed statement of purpose, multiple references, and a CV, with research degrees often requiring an additional research proposal. Finally, for undergraduate or postgraduate applications, you must demonstrate that you can cover tuition and living expenses for visa purposes, although postgraduate tuition fees tend to vary more significantly and may be higher, and for research programmes, you will often have access to additional funding opportunities such as scholarships.

The Universities and Colleges Admissions Service—better known as UCAS—is the central system that handles most applications for full-time undergraduate courses in the UK. It works as a single platform where you can apply to up to five courses, submit your personal statement, and provide references.

It's important to note that UCAS doesn't actually make any admissions decisions; it simply passes your application along to the universities you choose. As an international student, whether you need to use UCAS depends mainly on the type of course you're applying for. If you're applying for a full-time undergraduate degree (like a BA, BSc, or LLB), then UCAS is required. But if you're applying for a postgraduate programme—such as a master's or PhD—or for most part-time courses, you'll usually apply directly to the university instead.

A UCAS Personal Statement is your chance to show universities who you are, what motivates you, and why you're excited about your chosen subject. It's a short piece of writing—up to 4,000 characters or 47 lines—where you explain your academic interests and the experiences that have prepared you for further study. This is especially important for you as an international student, as it helps admissions tutors understand your background and how it connects to the UK system.

To write a strong statement, start by clearly explaining what draws you to your subject. Mention particular topics, questions, or ideas that fascinate you—especially those you may not have had the chance to study formally. Then show how you've explored your interest: through reading, online courses, competitions, research, or relevant work experience. These examples should demonstrate genuine curiosity and commitment.

If parts of your educational system differ from the UK approach—whether in curriculum, grading, or structure—you can briefly explain this so tutors can see your preparation in context.

Keep the focus mainly academic. Extracurricular activities are useful when they help show skills that matter for your course, such as analytical thinking, communication, resilience, or problem-solving. Whenever you mention an activity, reflect on what you learnt from it and how it equips you for university-level study.

Finally, aim for clear, concise writing with a logical flow. Proofread carefully and make sure your statement presents you as thoughtful, motivated, and ready for the next stage of your academic journey.

If you're applying to UK universities through UCAS, there are a few reliable ways to keep track of your application. The main place to check your progress is the UCAS Hub. Once you log in, you can see real-time updates—such as when UCAS receives your application, when it's forwarded to your chosen universities, and any decisions that universities make.

You'll also receive email updates from both UCAS and the universities you've applied to. Make sure your contact details are up to date and check your spam folder from time to time so you don't miss anything important. Universities might contact you directly if they need extra documents, want to schedule an interview, or have updates about your application.

If you're ever unsure about a delay, you can reach out to a university's admissions office and provide your UCAS ID so they can look into it. Some universities also have their own applicant portals, where you can see what documents they've received, what's still outstanding, and how far along your review is.

And if you run into any technical issues or just need a bit of help understanding the process, UCAS International Support is available to assist you and help make sure you always know what's happening with your application.

After you start getting your UCAS offers, it's important to know what each one means and what you need to do next. Universities can give you two types of offers: Conditional offers – you still need to meet certain requirements, like final exam grades, an English-language test, or submitting missing documents. Unconditional offers – you've already met all the entry requirements.

Once all your universities have replied, you'll need to choose your Firm choice (your top option) and, if you want, an Insurance choice as a backup. Make sure you reply before the UCAS deadline—if you miss it, UCAS will automatically decline all your offers.

You'll usually need to pay a tuition deposit soon after choosing your Firm choice. This deposit is often required before the university can issue your CAS (Confirmation of Acceptance for Studies). To get your CAS, you must meet all conditions, pass any pre-CAS checks, and send the university certain documents (like your passport or financial proof).

For the UK Student Visa, you'll need financial evidence that meets specific UKVI rules—this usually includes the 28-day bank statement requirement, so make sure your funds are ready well in advance. Upload your final academic results or English-test scores as soon as you have them to avoid any delays with your CAS or visa.

Once your CAS arrives, you can start your visa application, pay the required fees, and either attend a biometrics appointment or use the UKVI app (depending on your nationality). While waiting for your visa decision, you can also organise accommodation, follow any pre-arrival instructions from your university, and plan your travel.

If you don't meet the conditions of your Firm choice, the university might reconsider, your Insurance choice might become your place, or you could explore opportunities through Clearing.

Overall, the period after receiving offers is all about staying on top of deadlines, keeping documents ready, and preparing early for the deposit, CAS, and visa process so everything goes smoothly.

For almost all undergraduate degrees in the UK, you have to apply through UCAS. It's the standard national system, and nearly every university requires it. Only in rare or very specific cases—such as certain late-entry or non-standard programmes—might a university accept a direct application, but this isn't common and depends entirely on the institution.

Postgraduate applications are different: most universities handle those directly through their own online application portals, not UCAS. As an international student, you should generally plan on using UCAS for undergraduate courses unless a university clearly tells you otherwise.

You can apply directly to an Irish university, especially for postgraduate programmes and some undergraduate pathways that aren't processed through the CAO. Each university—like UCD, Trinity, DCU, Maynooth, UL, or MTU—has its own online application system. Some postgraduate programmes may instead use the Postgraduate Applications Centre (PAC).

You'll typically need documents such as transcripts, graduation certificates (and translations if needed), English-language test scores, a passport, and—if applying for postgraduate study—a CV, statement of purpose, and possibly references, a portfolio, or a research proposal. Deadlines vary a lot between universities and programmes. Many undergraduate international applications run on a rolling basis from around April to July, while postgraduate deadlines usually fall between January and June. These timelines are completely separate from CAO deadlines. After applying, the university will confirm they received your application, might ask for extra documents or an interview, and—if accepted—will send you a conditional or full offer along with steps for accepting it, paying any deposit, and starting your visa process.

The Central Applications Office (CAO) is Ireland's national system for applying to full-time undergraduate courses. Instead of sending separate applications to each university, you fill out one form and rank your course choices in order of preference.

If you're from the EU, EEA, or Switzerland, you'll almost always apply through the CAO for undergraduate programmes.

If you're a non-EU international student, you'll need to apply directly to the universities, unless a particular course specifically tells you to use the CAO. Postgraduate programmes never use the CAO. Since every institution sets its own rules, it's always best to check the exact instructions on the university's International Admissions page.

If you want to study in the UK and you're 16 or older, you'll need a Student visa (previously called the Tier 4 visa). To get this visa, you must have an offer from a school or university that's approved by the UK government. You'll also need to show that you can pay your tuition and living costs, meet the English-language requirements, and apply within the allowed timeframe.

If you're applying from outside the UK, you can submit your application no earlier than six months before your course starts—but it's smart to apply as soon as you receive your CAS (Confirmation of Acceptance for Studies). This gives you enough time to gather documents like financial evidence or TB test results, and helps avoid delays during busy periods.

Ideally, you should secure your CAS, apply for your visa promptly, allow for processing (usually 3–4 weeks), and plan your travel so you arrive up to a month before your course begins.

To meet the UK's financial requirement, you must show you have enough money to pay your tuition fees (as listed on your CAS) and cover your living costs.

You'll need to show:

  • £1,483 per month if your school is in London
  • £1,136 per month if your school is outside London

—up to 9 months max. Any deposits you already paid count toward the total.

You can prove your funds with:

  • personal or parental bank statements
  • bank letters from approved financial institutions
  • fixed-deposit certificates
  • an official sponsorship letter
  • or an accepted student-loan letter

If you're using a parent's bank account, you must also include a consent letter and proof of your relationship. The funds must have been in the account for 28 days, and the final statement date must be no more than 31 days before you apply. Documents not in English or Welsh need a certified translation. The UKVI may also verify your bank or sponsor details.

To qualify for a UK Student Visa, you must prove that you have enough money to support yourself while studying. The required amount depends on where you will study:

  • Studying in London: £1,529 per month
  • Studying outside London: £1,171 per month

These living costs are calculated for up to 9 months, even if your course is longer.

So the maximum you would need to show is:

  • London: £13,761
  • Outside London: £10,539

This is in addition to demonstrating that you can pay any outstanding tuition fees for your first year of study, as shown on your CAS.

You must usually show that you've held the required funds for 28 consecutive days, and your financial documents must be dated within 31 days of submitting your online visa application. Accepted evidence includes bank statements, official sponsorship letters, or approved student loans.

The Immigration Health Surcharge (IHS) is a compulsory fee you must pay as part of your Student Visa application. It gives you access to the UK's National Health Service (NHS) during your stay.

  • The current IHS fee for students (and their dependants) is £776 per year.
  • It's calculated based on the total length of your visa, including any additional time added before or after your course.
  • You pay the IHS online, during the visa application process.
  • Once paid, you'll receive an IHS reference number, which forms part of your application.

If the wrong amount is paid, UKVI may contact you for a correction; if it isn't resolved, the application can be rejected.

Yes—most Student Visa holders can work in the UK, but there are strict rules.

Working hours allowed

  • Degree-level students: Up to 20 hours per week during term time
  • Below degree level: Up to 10 hours per week during term time
  • During official university vacations: You may work full time

  • Professional sportsperson or entertainer roles

Always check the exact work permissions stated on your visa vignette or BRP, as breaking work conditions can affect your immigration status. After completing your course, you may be eligible for the Graduate Visa, which allows full-time work.

When your course finishes, your Student visa doesn't end right away. The UK gives international students some extra "wrap-up time" to let you prepare to leave or apply for a new visa. How much time you get depends on how long your course was:

  • 12 months or longer: usually 4 extra months
  • Between 6 and 12 months: usually 2 extra months
  • Short courses (non-pre-sessional): usually 7 days
  • Pre-sessional courses: usually 1 extra month

PhD students normally get the same long wrap-up period unless they apply for something separate, like the Doctorate Extension Scheme.

If you want to stay in the UK after this additional period, you must either leave before your visa expires or apply for a new visa—such as the Graduate visa—before your current one runs out.

Most importantly, your real permission end date is the one printed on your BRP or digital eVisa, which might not match your course end date. Always double-check it to avoid overstaying.

If your Student visa is refused, don't panic—here's what to do:

  1. Read the refusal letter carefully. It explains why your application was refused and lists any deadlines (like for an Administrative Review).
  2. Tell your university right away. Share the refusal letter with International Student Support or Admissions; they can advise you on your next steps and whether you'll need a new CAS or a different start date.
  3. Get proper immigration advice. A regulated immigration adviser or solicitor can help figure out whether the refusal was caused by missing evidence, a misunderstanding, or a simple mistake.
  4. Choose your next step:
    • Administrative Review – if you believe the Home Office made an error.
    • New application – if your documents need fixing or adding.

Take time to gather the right documents and keep your university updated as you move forward.

If you're planning to study full-time in Ireland and stay longer than 90 days, you'll usually need a Long Stay "D" Study Visa. This visa lets you:

  • live in Ireland for your programme
  • register for an Irish Residence Permit after arrival
  • work part-time during term and full-time during holidays (if your course allows)

To apply, you'll need a Letter of Acceptance from an approved institution, proof of tuition payment, evidence you can support yourself financially, medical insurance, and a clean immigration record.

If your course is shorter than 90 days, you may instead need a Short Stay "C" Study Visa. Some nationalities don't need an entry visa at all, but still must register if they stay over 90 days.

You should apply for your Irish Study Visa (Stamp 2) as soon as you have received your unconditional offer and gathered all required documents. Applications can be submitted up to four months before the course start date, and they should not be filed later than three weeks before classes begin, as late applications may be returned without processing. Because processing typically takes four to eight weeks—and can take longer during peak periods—it is strongly advisable to apply three to four months in advance to allow time for potential document checks or additional requests. Many visa offices process applications in the order they are received, so submitting early provides a helpful buffer. Finally, you should avoid booking flights or making any non-refundable travel plans until your visa has been approved and the visa sticker has been placed in your passport.

If you're applying for an Irish Stamp 2 student visa on or after 30 June 2025, you'll need to show that you have at least €10,000 available to support yourself for a full academic year.

If your course is shorter—6 to 8 months—the requirement is based on monthly funding:

  • €833 per month
  • About €4,998 for a six-month course
  • About €6,665 for an eight-month course

You must be able to show immediate access to these funds when you apply. If your program lasts more than one year, you'll need to prove you can access another €10,000 for each additional year (on top of tuition fees).

Processing times for Irish study visas can vary quite a bit depending on the time of year, the type of visa, and how complete your application is. As a general guide:

  • Long-stay ("D") study visas usually take around 8 weeks, but during busy periods—especially right before the academic year starts—it can stretch to 10–12 weeks.
  • Short-stay ("C") study visas are often quicker, typically 3–8 weeks, depending on the visa office handling your application.

Yes! You can stay in Ireland after graduation through the Third-Level Graduate Programme (Stamp 1G). This permission lets you remain in Ireland to look for work.

Here's how long you can stay:

  • NFQ Level 8 graduates: up to 12 months
  • NFQ Level 9 or 10 graduates: up to 24 months (normally issued as 12 months first, with the option to renew for another year if you've been actively looking for graduate-level employment)

To apply, you must:

Once you secure an appropriate job, you can switch to a longer-term work permission such as the Critical Skills Employment Permit.

Overall, the programme is one of the clearest routes for you to continue living and working in Ireland after your studies—just make sure you apply on time and meet the requirements.

In both the UK and Ireland, universities set their own tuition fees, and international students generally pay more because their studies aren't subsidised by the government. Instead, universities charge the full cost of providing the programme.

For undergraduate degrees, fees are normally fixed per academic year and depend heavily on the subject. Lab-based and clinical courses are typically more expensive. Some years, like placement or clinical years, might have different fee amounts.

For postgraduate programmes, you may be charged for the full programme upfront or on a yearly basis. Courses involving labs, research supervision, or specialist facilities often come with extra costs. Universities may also add mandatory charges such as lab or studio fees, fieldwork costs, or administrative fees.

Universities may guarantee your fee rate for the full duration of study—but not always—so it's important to check your official offer letter.

Yes — most universities across the UK and Ireland offer payment plans so students don't have to pay all their tuition fees at once. Instead, the yearly fee is usually split into a few instalments.

Both the UK and Ireland require international students to pay a deposit to secure their place.

UK universities often:

  • Invoice you after you accept your offer.
  • Require the deposit before issuing a CAS.
  • Ask you to pay the remaining balance before or at enrolment.
  • Offer instalment plans—commonly 50% at enrolment, then two smaller payments later in the academic year.

Irish universities generally:

  • Require payment just before or at registration.
  • Offer fewer instalment options—often 50% in September and 50% in January.

The UK and Irish universities accept tuition fee payments through similar payment methods: online portals, bank transfers, Flywire/Convera, or card payments.

It is advisable to plan transfers early and keep payment receipts because currency conversions and international bank transfers can take time.

Yes, you need health insurance to study in the UK or Ireland. However, the health insurance rules for international students work a bit differently in the UK and Ireland.

Studying in the UK

If your study program lasts more than six months, you'll need to pay the Immigration Health Surcharge (IHS) when you apply for your visa. This gives you access to the National Health Service (NHS) just like a UK resident—so most healthcare is covered.

If your program is six months or shorter, you won't pay the IHS. That means you're only entitled to emergency care, so having private health insurance is highly recommended.

Studying in the Republic of Ireland

If you're not from the EU/EEA or Switzerland, you must have private medical insurance that meets the country's immigration requirements. Travel insurance can work for your first year, but if you stay longer, you'll need to switch to an Irish-based policy.

If you're an EU/EEA student, your EHIC card will cover public healthcare, though many students still choose private insurance for extra protection.

In short

  • UK: Longer programs require paying the IHS for NHS access; shorter programs should have private insurance.
  • Ireland: Private medical insurance is required for most non-EU students, while EU/EEA students can use EHIC.
